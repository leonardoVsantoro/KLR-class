


\subsubsection*{B.2. Scalable approximation schemes for kernel-likelihood classifiers}  
The classification rule in Eq.~\eqref{eq:multiclass_rule} depends on the inversion and log-determinant computations of the embedded covariance operators $\S_{\bbP_j}$, whose computational costs scale as $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ with the sample size $n$. To make the approach scalable, I will employ {low-rank approximations} that effectively reduce this computational burden. In particular, I will explore the use of {Nyström approximations}, which subsample rows and columns of the kernel Gram matrix to construct a low-rank surrogate of the covariance embedding operator, and \emph{Random Fourier Features}, which provide an explicit low-dimensional feature map approximating a shift-invariant kernel via Monte Carlo approximations of its spectral representation.
Such approximation strategies (Nyström or RFF) impose an explicit rank constraint, effectively performing \emph{spectral pruning} that discards low-variance directions, which in turn entails an implicit regularisation that mitigates overfitting. 
 To further boost scalability and stability, I will combine them with {ensemble methods}: train \(B\) independent classifiers \(\{f_b\}_{b=1}^B\) on subsampled batches of size \(m\) and or feature dimension \(M\), and aggregate the corresponding classifiers.
This reduces per-model cost to \(\mathcal{O}(m^3)\) or \(\mathcal{O}(M^3)\), while averaging out variability introduced by subsampling and projection.
I will seek to both rigorously quantify the statistical loss and computational complexity introduced by these approximation schemes, and to develop a ready-to-use software package that enables their deployment in large-scale settings.
These investigations will form a central component of my research visit to prof. {Lorenzo Rosasco} at MaLGa.


\subsubsection*{B.3. New insights into benign overfitting and generalization in RKHS}
One adventurous direction aims to investigate whether the mutual singularity of Gaussian embeddings can shed light on why overfitted kernel methods often achieve perfect interpolation yet retain strong generalization, in particular in the context of classification. My central intuition is that kernel estimators may fit training data exactly because each class-conditional distrivution effectively occupies essentially separated affine subspaces in feature space; nonetheless, generalization can persist when the empirical and population distributions remain aligned in an appropriate sense. In this view, the singularity among embedded measures offers a principled explanation for benign overfitting phenomena in RKHSs, providing a fresh theoretical perspective on the generalization capacity of kernel-based learning.

\subsubsection{(C) Separation-of-measure at the frontier: optimality, Banach spaces, and graphical models}
\subsubsection*{C.1. Optimality of the Kernel likelihood ratio}
The singular behaviour of the {kernel} likelihood ratio \cite{santoroLRT25} (KLR) suggests that it may achieve statistical optimality in distinguishing probability distributions, analogous to the classical Neyman–Pearson likelihood ratio, but in an intrinsically nonparametric, infinite-dimensional setting. 
Furthermore, while invariant to smoothly invertible (diffeomorphisms) transformations of the likelihood ratio cannot increase statistical power, kernel embeddings are inherently unbounded operations. This opens the intriguing possibility -- supported by preliminary empirical evidence -- that nonlinear unbounded embeddings can, in specific regimes, \emph{strictly improve} power beyond the classical likelihood ratio.  
In this project, I will formalize this notion of optimality by (i) identifying conditions under which the power of the KLR approaches the oracle Neyman–Pearson power for exponential families, and (ii)
identify regimes where nonlinear kernel embeddings provably yield strict gains over structured alternatives defined by spectral decay. 
% I will derive minimax lower bounds for two-sample testing and show that the suitably tuned KLR attains these bounds.  
A key step in this analysis is to understand the rate of divergence of the regularized Gaussian relative entropy,
$
D_{\gamma,\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \S_{\bbP}) \,\big\|\, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \S_{\bbQ}) \right) \to \infty$  as  $\gamma \to 0$,
and to relate this behaviour to a suitable notion of separation between $\bbP$ and $\bbQ$ in the original space. This turns out to be a delicate question, tightly linked to the spectral properties of a certain difference operator on the RKHS.
These project aims to connect the novel separation-of-measure phenomenon exhibited by RKHS embeddings with decision theory, and clarify our understanding of how nonlinear embeddings in RKHS confer a genuine statistical advantage.
